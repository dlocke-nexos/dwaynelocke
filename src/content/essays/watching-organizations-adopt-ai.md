For most of my career, I wasn't building startups. I was helping organizations navigate change.

I've worked inside large consulting firms, software companies, universities, healthcare organizations, and enterprise transformation programs. I've spent years on ERP implementations, organizational readiness, cutovers, governance models, operating models, and technology adoption.

On the surface, these projects looked very different. A university moving to Workday. A hospital replacing legacy systems. A global company redesigning its business processes. A small consulting firm trying to scale. Different industries, different technologies, different budgets.

But over time, I noticed something. The same problems kept showing up. Not technology problems. People problems. Process problems. Dependency problems.

Organizations would spend millions implementing new software, but the software wasn't the bottleneck. The bottleneck was how work flowed through the organization — who made decisions, where knowledge lived, how information moved, and most importantly, what the organization depended on to function.

## For thirty years, businesses scaled through software

CRM systems. ERP systems. HR systems. Accounting systems. Project management systems. The goal was simple: capture information, standardize processes, create visibility.

That worked. Businesses became more organized, more measurable, more predictable. But something else happened too. Software digitized work — it didn't necessarily perform the work. Humans still sat in the middle of most processes.

A lead entered a CRM. A salesperson followed up. A customer submitted a request. A support rep responded. An invoice was generated. A finance team processed it. The systems got better. The work still relied heavily on people.

> When organizations adopted cloud software, they digitized information. When organizations adopt AI, they begin digitizing capability.

## What I'm seeing today feels different

For the first time in my career, technology isn't just documenting work. It's beginning to perform it. The question is no longer "how do we track this process?" It's becoming "does a human need to perform this process at all?"

Most businesses are still early in this. They're experimenting with ChatGPT, testing AI note takers, automating a few emails, creating content faster. Useful, but small. The bigger shift is happening underneath: businesses are starting to create digital workers.

Not robots. Not science fiction. Systems that can follow instructions, access information, make decisions within defined boundaries, and perform work consistently. Historically, if you wanted a task done repeatedly, you hired someone. Today there's another option.

The challenge is that most organizations aren't structured for it. They don't have documented processes, clear decision rules, or centralized knowledge — and they often don't know where their information lives. Before you can build a digital worker, you have to understand how the work is performed today. Ironically, that's the same challenge organizations faced during ERP implementations: before you could automate a process, you first had to understand it.

## From chatbots to an operating layer

One of the most interesting developments I've been experimenting with is agentic workflows. Instead of using AI as a chatbot, organizations are beginning to use it as an operating layer. A lead arrives. An agent qualifies it. Another enriches the information. Another schedules a meeting. Another prepares documentation. Another updates systems. The workflow continues whether a human is actively involved or not. That starts to look less like software and more like a digital workforce.

## The part I keep coming back to: memory

Not human memory. Organizational memory. Historically it lived inside people — and people leave, forget, interpret instructions differently, have good days and bad days. Organizations spent decades trying to solve this with documentation: SOPs, knowledge bases, training manuals, playbooks.

Today we're seeing a new evolution. Memory is becoming operational. Files like memory.md, system prompts, workflow instructions, process documentation, and decision trees are becoming a kind of organizational DNA. Instead of training a new employee every time, we can increasingly train a digital worker once — and it executes the process repeatedly, consistently, without forgetting or improvising.

## This doesn't mean humans disappear

Far from it. What changes is the nature of human work. People increasingly focus on:

- Designing systems
- Defining outcomes
- Managing exceptions
- Improving processes
- Making strategic decisions

The repetitive work moves elsewhere. This is where I believe we're entering a new stage of organizational maturity — from software-enabled organizations to AI-enabled ones. Some businesses will make the transition quickly. Others will struggle. Most won't struggle because of technology. They'll struggle because their operating model was designed for a previous era.

Over the coming months, I want to explore this openly. Not as an AI expert. Not as someone claiming to have all the answers. But as someone who has spent years watching organizations evolve and now finds himself observing the next major shift. The question isn't whether AI will change how organizations scale. The question is how.

Let's figure it out together.
