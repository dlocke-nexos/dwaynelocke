For most of my career, implementing technology followed a fairly predictable pattern.

Whether it was Workday, Salesforce, Oracle, ServiceNow, or another enterprise platform, the process looked roughly the same. You selected software. You configured software. You integrated software. You tested software. You deployed software. Then you maintained software.

Different products. Different industries. Different project plans. But fundamentally, the deployment model was consistent.

The capability already existed. Your job was to choose it, configure it, and implement it.

If you wanted a CRM, you didn't build a CRM. You selected Salesforce. If you wanted HR software, you didn't build HR software. You selected Workday. If you wanted ITSM, you selected ServiceNow.

Most technology projects were exercises in selection and implementation. The software company produced the capability. The customer adopted it.

What I'm beginning to notice with AI is that this model no longer fully applies.

The technology is still important. The platforms are still important. But increasingly, organizations are not buying capability. They're producing capability.

That feels like a fundamental shift.

## The old deployment model

Historically, technology deployment looked something like this:

**Traditional SaaS deployment**

Select → Configure → Integrate → Test → Deploy → Maintain

The software vendor created the capability. The customer deployed it.

AI changes this — because the capability often doesn't exist until you create it.

An AI SDR doesn't arrive fully formed. An AI customer service agent doesn't arrive fully formed. An AI finance analyst doesn't arrive fully formed.

The organization has to design it. Train it. Refine it. Evaluate it. Govern it. Operate it.

The capability is increasingly created during implementation rather than purchased beforehand.

That may sound like a small distinction. I don't think it is. I think it's one of the biggest differences between the SaaS era and the AI era.

For the last twenty years, software vendors largely owned capability creation. Organizations owned deployment. Now those responsibilities are becoming blurred.

The organization itself becomes responsible for producing capability. The AI platform becomes infrastructure. The capability becomes the thing you build on top of it.

## A different lifecycle

This is why I think the deployment lifecycle is changing.

**AI deployment model**

Architect → Generate → Wire → Evaluate → Test → Deploy → Maintain

The first stage is new: **Architect**.

Before anything is built, organizations must determine what role is being performed, what decisions need to be made, what information is required, what tools are available, and what guardrails exist.

This feels much closer to organizational design than traditional software implementation.

The second stage is even more interesting: **Generate**.

In traditional software, capability was purchased. In AI systems, capability is increasingly generated.

Prompts. Instructions. Knowledge. Memory. Tools. Agent behaviors. The organization produces the capability itself.

Then comes wiring: connecting systems, APIs, knowledge sources, data, actions, and permissions.

## Evaluation as a discipline

The stage that fascinates me most is **evaluation**.

I think evaluation may become one of the defining disciplines of the AI era because AI systems are fundamentally different from traditional software.

Traditional software is largely deterministic. Given the same inputs, it produces the same outputs. AI systems do not always behave this way.

They require judgment, testing, observation, and measurement. Trust must be earned rather than assumed.

Organizations must prove capability, not just deploy it.

> In traditional software, defects are repaired. In AI systems, defects are often regenerated. The loop isn't always Build → Fix → Build. Increasingly it becomes Generate → Evaluate → Regenerate.

## Governance moves to the center

One of my current hypotheses is that governance is moving from the sidelines into the center of implementation.

Historically governance often sat above the project. AI changes that.

Governance increasingly becomes part of the capability itself: rules, boundaries, permissions, memory, escalation paths, and evaluation criteria.

These are no longer external controls. They are embedded directly into how digital workers operate.

The capability evolves through iteration rather than correction.

## Organizational design, not software projects

I don't think most organizations have fully adjusted to this shift yet.

Many are still approaching AI implementations as if they were traditional software deployments. What they're actually doing is creating capability. And creating capability requires a different operating model.

The more I observe AI implementations, the less they resemble software projects and the more they resemble organizational design projects.

You're defining roles. Designing behaviors. Creating knowledge structures. Establishing governance. Determining accountability. Building memory. Creating workers.

Some happen to be human. Some happen to be digital.

I don't know exactly where this leads. But I suspect we're moving from a world where organizations deployed software to a world where organizations increasingly deploy capability.

That feels like a bigger shift than most people realize.
