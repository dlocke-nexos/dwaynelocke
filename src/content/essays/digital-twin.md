For fifteen years, my job was to move an organization's work into software — ERP, HR, the big Workday rollouts.

We took what lived in employees' and business leaders' heads and put it into systems. Companies got more organized. More measurable. More scalable.

But mostly, the software just made the process electronic. Paper became screens. Steps became rules. It captured how the work was done. It never did the work. A person still did.

That's the line that's about to move.

> Software digitized information. AI digitizes capability.

In an [earlier essay](/) I drew that distinction. Organizations are already living it — building digital workers that follow instructions, decide inside set boundaries, and do repeatable work without forgetting.

What I didn't say then is where it goes next. It doesn't stop at the company. It comes home.

## I'm digitizing myself

For the first time, I'm not digitizing an organization's capability. I'm digitizing mine.

I've been building a digital twin of myself. Not a chatbot that sounds vaguely like me. A system that writes in my voice, thinks in my frameworks, drafts my emails the way I'd write them, prioritizes my day the way I would, builds models the way I build them, and produces work I'd put my name on.

It's early. Today it's me, a set of instructions, and a model turning them into drafts. But the direction is clear.

## A repeatable lifecycle

Here's the pattern I'm seeing — and the human never leaves the loop.

**Capture.** Teach it who you are. Your voice. Your history. Your judgment. The stories that make your output *yours*. This is the onboarding, and it's the hard part — most of us have never written down how we actually operate.

**Configure.** Connect it to how you work. Your tools. Your files. Your standards. It has to reach for the same things you reach for.

**Operate.** Let it produce. In your style, to your bar. You review. You correct. You approve.

**Maintain.** The stage people underestimate. A twin isn't built once. You feed it continuously so it stays aligned with who you're becoming, not who you were.

Capture and Configure get it running. Operate and Maintain keep it *you*.

## It doesn't stop at one kind of work

Whatever you do, the twin learns your version of it. In marketing, it writes the post in your voice. In sales, it pitches the way you know converts and builds the deck that closes. In finance, it drafts the invoice and chases what's owed. In service, it onboards the customer and routes the ticket the way you would. In HR, it writes the job description and screens the way you hire. In IT, it sets up the security groups and stages the data-load files the way you'd configure them.

Trained once. Running across the whole business. Every task in your style, to your standard.

## Doesn't this replace you?

No. It extends you.

You stay the decision-maker. You set direction, handle the exceptions, own the judgment. The twin takes the repetition — consistently, without forgetting, without drifting — as long as you keep feeding it and watching it.

It's the same lesson from every transformation I've worked on. The technology doesn't remove the human. It changes what the human spends their time on.

## This becomes a product

Not today. But I can see it. An onboarding that helps anyone teach a tool who they are — their history, their work, their voice, the way they think — and stand up their own digital twin. Then a way to maintain it, so it keeps producing as they would, with them still watching.

We spent thirty years digitizing companies. The next phase is more personal. We're going to digitize ourselves.

I'm doing it in the open — building mine, learning what holds and what breaks. If that's a road you're walking too, let's figure it out together.
